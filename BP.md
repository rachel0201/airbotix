# Airbotix — Business Plan

**Pre-Seed Round | May 2026**

> *AI coding will be the universal literacy of the next generation. Airbotix is building the K-12 curriculum and platform to deliver it.*

---

## Executive Summary

Airbotix is a K-12 AI coding and robotics education company. We believe AI coding is on a trajectory to become as fundamental as reading, writing, and English literacy were for previous generations — and the K-12 education system is not equipped to deliver it.

We have run a focused pilot with **100+ students across 2 partner schools in Australia**, validating that age-appropriate AI coding curriculum works in the classroom, that parents pay for it, and that schools want recurring relationships rather than one-off workshops.

We are now raising a **A$150K validation round** — deliberately not a scale round — to:

1. **Hire a school partnerships lead** to grow the school pipeline (the real bottleneck at this stage).
2. Build the AI coding platform MVP, with founders covering primary engineering.
3. Onboard our existing 100+ students as cohort 1; expand to 5–7 partner schools.
4. Validate the unit economics (subscription + LLM token margin) with real usage data.
5. Earn a proper Seed (A$1–2M) on validated metrics, not promises.

This is an honest pre-seed story: small validated pilot, focused capital plan, large secular tailwind, founders with relevant operating experience.

## 1. The Thesis: AI Coding as Universal Literacy

Every generation has had a defining literacy:

| Era | Defining Skill |
|-----|----------------|
| 1900s | Reading and writing |
| 1980s–90s | Operating a computer |
| 2000s–10s | Using the internet |
| 2020s and beyond | **Coding with AI** |

This is not speculation. It is observable today:

- The most productive software engineers are those who collaborate fluently with AI agents — and the productivity gap with non-AI engineers widens every quarter.
- Universities, research labs, and major employers are restructuring graduate hiring around AI fluency.
- Roles in law, medicine, design, finance, and operations now expect employees to build with AI tools, not just consume them.

Children born in 2020 will graduate university in 2042. By then, the ability to direct, code with, and build on top of AI systems will be table stakes for almost every knowledge profession — much as competency with computers and the internet is today.

**The K-12 education system is not ready.** Curriculum updates lag a decade behind technology shifts. Most teachers are not trained on AI tools. Existing coding-for-kids platforms (Scratch, Tynker, Code.org) are pre-AI in design and pedagogy. Generic LLMs (ChatGPT, Claude) are designed for adults, with no scaffolding for learners.

This is the gap Airbotix exists to close.

---

## 2. The Problem

### For students
- Generic LLMs treat children like adults. There is no project structure, no skill progression, no celebration of milestones, no age-appropriate content.
- Existing kids' coding platforms teach pre-AI skills. They are not preparing students for the workflows that will define the next 30 years.
- Children who use AI without structure learn to consume answers, not build systems. They become passive users of AI rather than creators with it.

### For parents
- Parents understand AI literacy will matter for their children. They do not have a clear, structured path to provide it.
- Private tutoring is expensive (A$80–150/hour) and not AI-native.
- Online platforms are not localized to Australian curriculum, do not offer parent visibility, and are not designed around AI-fluent project work.

### For schools
- Australia's Digital Technologies F–10 curriculum mandates digital and computational literacy. Most schools have no concrete AI implementation plan.
- Existing teachers are not trained on AI pedagogy. There are no off-the-shelf, classroom-ready AI coding programs from established providers.
- Schools want a partner, not just a vendor. They are looking for end-to-end programs they can adopt with confidence.

A focused 3-year goal: **5,000–10,000 paying students** across school partnerships and direct-to-family subscriptions, equivalent to a single-digit-million-dollar ARR business — with a path to 10× from there as the product matures and international cohorts open.

## 4. Why Now

Four converging tailwinds make this the right moment to build:

1. **AI capability**: Frontier LLMs are now powerful enough to act as personalized tutors, project partners, and code reviewers — at scale, in real time.
2. **AI cost**: Inference costs have fallen ~95% in 18 months. K-12 consumer pricing is finally viable.
3. **Curriculum reform**: Australia's Digital Technologies F-10 curriculum and similar reforms in other jurisdictions are creating regulatory pull for AI-aligned content.
4. **Demand acceleration**: Post-ChatGPT, parental awareness of AI's role in their children's future has accelerated dramatically. AI-related education is moving up the household priority list.

The category window is open now. Within 5 years, K-12 AI education will have a small set of category-defining brands. Our goal is to be one of them — anchored in Australia, expanding into the global Chinese-speaking market.

## 5. Our Approach

We have deliberately built from curriculum outward, not from product inward. Our architecture is a three-layer stack:

### Layer 1 — Curriculum and Workshops (today)

- Hands-on programs combining AI concepts with physical robotics (mBot, sensor-based projects, generative AI exploration).
- Delivered as in-school workshops and study tours.
- Currently running in 2 partner schools, with ~100+ students taught to date.
- This layer validates content quality, parent willingness to pay, and school adoption mechanics.

### Layer 2 — Kids-Safe AI Coding LLM (next 12 months)

A purpose-built coding environment for K-12 students, built on top of frontier LLMs (Claude, OpenAI) but wrapped in three things a raw chatbot does not provide:

- **A kids-tailored AI experience** — age-appropriate scaffolding, project-based progression, encouragement of building and creating rather than passive Q&A. Sparks imagination instead of producing answers.
- **Safety guardrails parents trust** — content moderation, topic boundaries, age-tier behavioural settings. No raw model exposure. Families subscribe; we handle the API plumbing — parents never configure keys, manage usage, or worry about what their kids are typing into ChatGPT.
- **Parent visibility layer** — progress visibility, project portfolios, weekly summaries. Parents see exactly what their kids are building.

Monetisation: **subscription + LLM token margin**. The kid gets a safe creative space, the parent gets peace of mind, we get recurring revenue plus margin upside as inference costs continue falling.

### Layer 3 — Hackathons and University Pipeline (12–24 months)

- Quarterly student hackathons across Australia, expanding regionally.
- Top student portfolios connected to university admissions pathways through partner relationships.
- Particularly compelling for overseas Chinese families, where college admissions outcomes are a primary purchase driver.

Each layer reinforces the next. Workshops drive platform signups. The platform produces hackathon-ready students. Hackathons produce alumni stories that drive workshop demand. The result is a compounding, content-rich brand rather than a thin software product.

## 6. Business Model

We expect blended gross margins in the 65–75% range, with four revenue streams:

| Stream | Pricing | Margin Profile | Role |
|--------|---------|----------------|------|
| **B2C subscription** | A$29–79/month per student | High (~80%) | Recurring base |
| **LLM token resale** | Markup over wholesale API cost | Medium (~30–50%) | Margin upside as usage scales |
| **B2B school licensing** | Annual per-school or per-student license | High (~85%+) | High-LTV anchor accounts |
| **Workshops & hackathons** | Per-event fees, sponsorships | Medium (~50%) | Acquisition + brand + cash flow |

The token resale model is intentionally non-headline. It is a margin enhancer, not a thesis. Subscription and B2B licensing are the durable revenue base.

Workshops are not a cost center — they are our primary user acquisition channel. Compared to typical EdTech CACs (A$150–300 per family via paid digital), workshop-led acquisition is materially cheaper, more durable, and produces stronger retention.

## 8. Go-to-Market

Our GTM strategy unfolds in three stages:

### Stage 1 — Workshop-led acquisition (now)
Schools and families are introduced to Airbotix through in-school workshops and study tours. Workshop graduates and their families are the first cohort for the upcoming platform.

### Stage 2 — Local platform expansion (Year 1)
Convert workshop graduates into platform subscribers. Expand from 2 to ~20 partner schools across the Sydney/Melbourne/Brisbane corridors. Pilot the parent dashboard layer.

### Stage 3 — International cohort and B2B (Year 2+)
Launch first targeted cohort for overseas Chinese-speaking families (Australia and Southeast Asia diaspora first; North America later). Begin direct school district conversations for B2B licensing. Establish first university partnership for the hackathon-to-admissions pipeline.

The flywheel: workshops generate students → students become platform subscribers → platform produces hackathon participants → hackathon outcomes attract new families and schools → repeat.

| **Code.org** | Free, broad reach, classroom focus | Not AI-native; no parent visibility; no Australian curriculum specificity |

Our differentiated position rests on:

2. **Robotics + AI dual modality** — physical projects anchor the abstract, which improves retention and parent perception of value.
3. **Australian curriculum alignment** — a real procurement requirement for AU schools.
4. **Parent visibility as a first-class product surface** — not bolted on.
5. **Cross-border GTM** — Australia as a base, with a credible path to overseas Chinese-speaking families.
6. **Hackathon-to-admissions pipeline** — a strong outcome story that few competitors can match.

## 12. Use of Funds & The Ask

We are raising **A$150K as a validation round** to provide ~9 months of focused runway. This is deliberately **not a scale round**. Its job is narrow: hire a school partnerships lead to grow the school network while founders build product, prove unit economics on the resulting cohort, and earn a proper Seed (A$1–2M) on real data. Founders forgo or minimise salary at this stage; the capital goes to BD, product, content, and operations.

### Indicative allocation (A$150K)

The single biggest line item is a **school partnerships lead** — the bottleneck at this stage isn't engineering (founders cover that); it's a dedicated person walking into schools, talking to principals, and signing pilot agreements.

- **40% (~A$60K) — School partnerships lead + BD travel**: A part-time or fractional partnerships lead (or commission-based deal); school visits; sample materials for pitches.
- **25% (~A$37K) — Product & engineering**: AI coding platform MVP, parent dashboard. Founders (Joe ex-Google, Lightman AI engineer) build the primary surfaces; selective contractors for design and integration work.
- **15% (~A$22K) — Curriculum & content production**: AI-aligned curriculum across 3 age tiers; teacher-facing materials; content review.
- **10% (~A$15K) — Workshop materials + ops**: mBots and sensors, transport, on-site delivery.
- **10% (~A$15K) — Infrastructure + LLM API + compliance**: Hosting, frontier-LLM API spend for cohort 1, AU Privacy Act / eSafety / child-data safeguards, legal entity, insurance, buffer.

### Milestones for the next 9 months

- **School partnerships lead hired in month 1** — owns the school BD pipeline.
- Platform MVP live; existing 100+ students as cohort 1.
- **5–7 partner schools** (BD-driven expansion from current 2).
- **100–200 paying student subscriptions** with retention data.
- **Token margin model validated** — clear unit economics on inference cost vs. subscription pricing.
- Path to **A$200–400K ARR**, ready to raise a proper Seed (A$1–2M).
